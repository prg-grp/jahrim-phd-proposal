
% CONTEXT: What is software verification?
\textit{Software verification} is the discipline of assuring that a software system
conforms to a given specification. It is a traditional area of research in 
computer science, drawing on both \textit{software engineering} and 
\textit{programming languages}.
\textit{Dynamic verification}, such as \textit{testing} \cite{ammann2016testing} and 
\textit{fuzzing} \cite{miller1990fuzzing}, 
executes the system under selected configurations to discover deviations from 
the specification, i.e., \textit{bugs}. 
Conversely, \textit{static verification}, such as \textit{model checking} \cite{clarke2018modelchecking}
and \textit{theorem proving} \cite{harrison2009theoremproving}, analyzes all possible behaviors of the
system
to guarantee that it always satisfies the specification. Since this is rarely
feasible for complex systems, static verification often relies on
\textit{abstractions} that approximate the system and the specification to make
the analysis tractable. In that sense, it is \textit{static} because the 
analysis is performed without executing the original system.

% FOCUS: What is theorem proving?
In this proposal, we focus on \textit{static verification} and specifically on
\textit{theorem proving} for software verification. A theorem prover is a tool
that checks the validity of \textit{formulas} in some \textit{logic}~\cite{kroening2016decision}, 
e.g., whether a \textit{goal} property (i.e., some behavior) is implied from
a set of known facts (i.e., the system). 
To do so, theorem provers can apply a mix of two approaches: the 
\textit{model-theoretic} approach cleverly enumerates all possible
models of the facts and checks if the goal holds, i.e., searches for
the first system configuration that invalidates the expected behavior; the 
\textit{proof-theoretic} approach derives the goal from the 
known facts using the set of valid inference rules of the logic, i.e., 
transforms the system into a logically equivalent system for which the property 
trivially holds. 

% SIGNIFICANCE: Why is theorem proving important?
The guarantees provided by static verification are essential to determine which
conditions are \textit{sufficient} or \textit{necessary} for a system to behave 
as expected. 
For example, a \textit{type system} like Rust's enforces 
\textit{ownership semantics} in a program, which is a sufficient condition for
memory safety and data race freedom \cite{jung2018rustbelt}. 
More expressive analyzers, such as the Viper \textit{verification language}~\cite{mueller2016viper} can
check that given \textit{preconditions} are sufficient to guarantee arbitrary
\textit{postconditions} of a program, based on \textit{separation logic}~\cite{reynolds2002separation} and
\textit{SMT solvers}~\cite{demoura2008z3}. 
At the boundary of expressiveness, \textit{proof assistants} like Lean can check
the proofs of arbitrary properties of the system using \textit{dependent type theory}
\cite{demoura2021lean4}, but they enforce \textit{totality} of definitions, which is a
necessary condition for the soundness of the proofs.
Together, these tools provide us with a better understanding of the system
before it is deployed in the real world, preventing costly failures \cite{lions1996ariane}. 
Their relevance is even more pronounced with the surge in software produced by
\textit{generative artificial intelligence}, which has fueled a push to certify
machine-generated artifacts \cite{sun2024clover,yang2023leandojo,song2025leancopilot,thakur2025clever,cook2025autoreasoning,poesia2022synchromesh,nagy2026chopchop,mundler2025typeconstrained}.

% LIMITATIONS: What are the challenges in theorem proving?
Theorem proving faces two fundamental challenges: \textit{scalability} and
\textit{usability}. Scalability concerns the computational cost of the analysis:
the space of models to enumerate or proofs to derive grows rapidly, and is often
undecidable, so a prover may fail to establish a goal within any practical time
and memory budget. Usability concerns the human cost: expressing a system and
its specification in a formal logic, and steering the prover toward a proof,
demands considerable expertise and manual effort. These two challenges are in
tension, and theorem provers are commonly classified by how they trade one
against the other, according to their level of automation. \textit{Automated
theorem provers} (ATPs) require no human guidance and thus minimize the
usability burden, but their automation reaches only a limited class of goals
before the search becomes intractable; their central challenge is therefore
scalability. \textit{Interactive theorem provers} (ITPs) admit human guidance
and can, in principle, prove arbitrary properties, so expressiveness is not the
wall; their central challenge is instead the human effort this guidance
requires, i.e., usability.

% INSIGHT: Most verification tools eventually rely on e-graphs. ATPs use it for
% equational reasoning and ITPs use it for automated proof search.
Underlying much of this ecosystem is a single data structure: the
\textit{e-graph}, which compactly represents a congruence relation over terms
and originates in the congruence-closure decision procedures of Nelson and
Oppen~\cite{nelson1980congruence}. Both families of provers reduce core
reasoning tasks to operations on e-graphs (Figure~\ref{fig:pyramid}), though to
different ends. ATPs use
them for \textit{equational reasoning}: SMT solvers rely on congruence closure
and e-matching to combine theories and instantiate quantifiers
\cite{demoura2007ematching, demoura2008z3}, while \textit{equality-saturation}
engines make the e-graph the central representation for applying rewrites
without committing to an order \cite{tate2009eqsat, willsey2021egg}. ITPs use
them for \textit{automated proof search}: tactics such as the \texttt{grind}
tactic in Lean discharge goals by saturating an e-graph with the available facts
and their congruence consequences~\cite{leangrind}. Because the same
structure sits beneath so many otherwise disconnected tools, an improvement to
the e-graph itself can propagate as an optimization across the whole ecosystem.

% CONTRIBUTION: Extend e-graphs to semantic-reasoning to enable optimizations
% across the entire theorem proving ecosystem.
At its core, this proposal extends the e-graph beyond the equalities it was
designed to represent, enriching it into a structure that reasons about a 
broader class of relationships between terms. We start from one of the most 
common patterns in theorem proving, \textit{proof by cases}, and show how it can
be made first-class in the e-graph. A proof by cases splits the goal into multiple subgoals, proven under mutually exclusive 
assumptions. Two ingredients are essential to this technique:
\textit{disequality reasoning}, which tracks disequalities arising from mutually
exclusive cases, and \textit{conditional reasoning}, which tracks which facts 
hold under which assumptions. With disequality reasoning, contradictions can be detected early, which can be exploited to prune the search space or in proof by contradiction. With conditional reasoning, the entailment of conditions can be mapped to a dependency relation among e-graphs, which can be exploited for
\textit{lazy reasoning}, deferring computation until a query demands it, and for
\textit{parallel reasoning}, scheduling proof exploration across independent
tasks.

% IMPACT: An optimized foundation for theorem proving enables more and faster
% automated proofs, which in turn increase both scalability for ATPs and 
% usability of ITPs.
Because these extensions live in the e-graph, the shared substrate of the
ecosystem, their benefits are not confined to a single tool: any prover built on
e-graphs stands to prove more, and to prove it faster. These gains fall along the
two axes that divide the field. For ATPs, detecting contradictions
early, deferring computation until it is needed, and exploring independent
branches in parallel all attack the combinatorial blow-up of the search,
helping with the scalability that most limits them. For ITPs,
absorbing disequalities and conditional facts into the e-graph lets automation
discharge goals that would otherwise fall to the user, helping with the usability
that most limits them. Because the improvement is made once, at the foundation,
and inherited by every tool above it, its impact compounds as the demand for
verified software continues to grow.
